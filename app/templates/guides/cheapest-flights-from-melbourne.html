    Melbourne Airport (MEL) is Australia's second-busiest international gateway and, on many routes, one of the most competitive. The combination of high passenger volumes and multiple competing carriers means fares on popular routes genuinely swing — sometimes by AUD $300–500 in a matter of days. That creates real opportunities for travellers who know how to read the market.

    The challenge is that most price comparison tools show you what a fare costs today, not whether that price is any good. A Melbourne–London fare at $1,400 might be the cheapest it's been in four months — or it might be $200 above where it sat last week. Without the historical context, you're guessing.

    Melbourne's Most Price-Volatile Routes

    Not all Melbourne routes swing equally. The routes where monitoring pays off most are:

    • MEL–LHR (London): The widest absolute price swing of any Melbourne route — a genuine sale can be AUD $600 below the typical fare. Qantas, Emirates, Singapore Airlines, and Qatar all compete aggressively, particularly in the shoulder season windows (March–May, September–October).
    • MEL–LAX (Los Angeles): Qantas operates the only non-stop MEL–LAX service, but competition from Air New Zealand and United via Auckland and San Francisco keeps prices in check. Flash sales on this route tend to be short and sharp — 24–48 hours.
    • MEL–NRT/HND (Tokyo): Japan Airlines and ANA both fly Melbourne direct, and both run periodic flash sales. The route is seasonal — prices spike during Japanese holiday periods but can be genuinely cheap in June and September.
    The flash sale window: Airline promotional fares rarely last more than 48–72 hours. They're released quietly — no press release, no announcement — and disappear when the allocated seats fill. The only reliable way to catch them is automated monitoring that checks prices continuously, not once a day or once a week.

    When Prices Are Lowest from Melbourne

    London and Europe

    April and May are the sweet spot for Melbourne–London fares: European summer hasn't yet pushed prices up, and Australian Easter crowds have moved on. Conversely, July–August (European peak and Australian school holidays combined) is the most expensive time on the route. Book at least 6 months in advance if you must travel in peak season.

    Japan

    The two periods to avoid are cherry blossom (late March–April) and Golden Week (late April–early May). June, September, and October offer the best combination of lower fares and pleasant weather. Japan Airlines and ANA both run off-peak promotional sales worth watching.

    United States

    January through March (post-holiday, pre-spring) sees the lowest MEL–LAX fares most years. American summer (June–August) and Australian school holidays in July push prices up on both the non-stop Qantas service and the one-stop alternatives.

    Asia and Bali

    Singapore and Bangkok routes run flash sales throughout the year — timing is less predictable than seasonality. Bali fares from Melbourne are reasonable year-round but peak noticeably in July and December school holidays.

    Avalon Airport — Worth Considering?

    Avalon Airport (AVV), about 55km southwest of Melbourne, handles some international charter and limited scheduled services but is not a meaningful alternative for the major routes listed above. For practical purposes, MEL is Melbourne's international departure point.
